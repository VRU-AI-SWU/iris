## Research Question
What skills do Thai employers prioritise, and what skill gaps exist between what new graduates possess and what employers require?

## Limitations of Existing Methods
Prior Thai skill gap studies relied on aggregate national statistics or small-scale qualitative interviews; no large-sample survey of Thai HR managers on skill priorities existed with cross-sector breakdown.

## Contribution
Survey of 310 HR managers and recruiters across Thai industries. Skill priority ranking derived from employer perspective. Quantification of skill gaps by firm size and sector using ANOVA.

## Proposed Method
Online questionnaire distributed to HR managers and recruiters (n = 310). Descriptive statistics (percentage, mean, SD) and One-Way ANOVA for cross-group comparisons. Thammasat University, Bangkok.

## Key Findings
Thai employers prioritise skills in this order: cognitive skills > social skills > emotional intelligence > thinking skills > growth mindset > information and digital literacy > communication > creativity. Significant variation in skill demand across firm sizes. New Thai graduates face skill mismatches — qualifications do not align with employer requirements.

## Limitations of This Paper
Survey-based — employer self-report of priorities, not observed from actual job postings. May not capture rapidly emerging technical skills (e.g., AI, cloud) that appear in job ads before HR survey responses catch up. English/Thai bilingual terminology not addressed.

## Notes for Iris
Provides Thai employer perspective to ground our market-side analysis. The skill priority ordering confirms cognitive and social skills dominate Thai employer discourse — but these are the soft skills we've explicitly excluded from v1. The technical/digital skills layer (information and digital literacy) is where our work adds value. The firm size variation suggests our industry segment breakdown is important: large vs SME employers may demand very different technical skill profiles.
