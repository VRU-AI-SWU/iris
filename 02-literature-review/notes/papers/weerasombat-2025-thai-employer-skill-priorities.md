## Research Question
What skills do Thai employers prioritise, and what are the actual skill gaps between what employers demand and what Thai senior students (supply side) self-assess as their skill level?

## Limitations of Existing Methods
Prior Thai skill gap studies relied on aggregate national statistics or small-scale qualitative interviews. No large-sample quantitative study existed that compared Thai employer skill demand directly against student-assessed skill supply across a validated 8-skill framework. Research in developing-country / Southeast Asian context remains scarce.

## Contribution
Largest Thai employer survey on skill priorities to date: 310 HR managers/recruiters across firm sizes (small < 200 employees, medium 200–500, large > 500). Matched with supply-side data from 350 Thammasat University social science students. Quantified gaps by skill type and tested firm-size effects via One-Way ANOVA. Empirically validated 8-skill framework for Thai context.

## Proposed Method
- **Demand side**: Online questionnaire to 310 HR managers/recruiters (March–July 2023); purposive + snowball sampling via PMAT (Personnel Management Association of Thailand); Likert scale 1–5
- **Sample composition**: 95 small (30.7%), 41 medium (13.2%), 174 large (56.1%) firms
- **Supply side**: 350 Thammasat 4th-year social science students; self-evaluation survey using same 8-skill framework
- **Analysis**: Descriptive statistics + One-Way ANOVA for firm-size comparison
- **8 skill sets**: (1) Thinking skills, (2) Information & digital literacy, (3) Social skills, (4) Communication & language, (5) Creativity & innovation, (6) EQ, (7) Growth mindset, (8) Cognitive skills
- **Validation**: IOC index (0.5–1.0); Cronbach's α 0.964–0.970 (high internal consistency)

## Key Findings
**Employer skill priority order** (demand, highest to lowest):
Cognitive > Social > EQ > Thinking > Growth mindset > Information & digital literacy > Communication & language > Creativity & innovation

**Skill gap size** (demand minus supply, largest to smallest gap):
Thinking (largest) > EQ > Communication & language > Information & digital literacy > Creativity & innovation > Growth mindset > Cognitive > Social (smallest)

- Significant firm-size effect confirmed: ANOVA p = 0.009 — large firms demand different skill profiles than small firms
- Large firms emphasise specialised technical skills, digital literacy, and managerial skills more; smaller firms prioritise versatility
- Social skills show the smallest gap (students rate themselves fairly high) but are among the most prioritised
- Thinking skills show the largest gap — Thai graduates systematically underperform employer expectations on analytical/critical thinking

## Limitations of This Paper
Survey-based (employer self-report) — may not reflect actual skill requirements seen in job postings; positivity bias possible. Supply-side sample is social science students at Thammasat only — not representative of CS/IT graduates. Focuses on soft/generic skills only — no technical or digital skills at granular level. English/Thai bilingual terminology not addressed.

## Notes for Iris
**Two relevant implications:**
1. **Soft skills dominate employer discourse, but technical skills are where Iris adds value**: The top 8 skills in this survey are all soft/generic — the technical/digital layer (information & digital literacy, rank 6) is where our skill extraction from job postings will produce the most differentiated and actionable insight. This confirms our v1 scope of focusing on technical skill gaps rather than generic soft skills.
2. **Firm size matters for skill demand**: The significant firm-size ANOVA effect (p = 0.009) means our gap reports should ideally segment by employer type. Consider adding a company-size flag to our job posting scraping pipeline so we can compare skill demand for large vs. SME Thai employers separately. This is a potential feature for Iris v2.
