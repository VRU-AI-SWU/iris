## Research Question
What is the current state of understanding and measurement of skill gaps in the context of Industry 4.0, and what methodological approaches have been used?

## Limitations of Existing Methods
No common definition of "skill gap" exists across the literature — terms like skill gap, skill mismatch, and skill shortage are used interchangeably with different meanings. Prior reviews are either regional or sector-specific; no comprehensive synthesis across measurement approaches existed. Survey-based methods dominate but have known weaknesses (subjectivity, peer positivity bias).

## Contribution
PRISMA-compliant systematic review of 40 articles (2012–2022) across ProQuest, ScienceDirect, Scopus, and Web of Science. Provides a unifying definition, taxonomy of measurement approaches, and identification of research gaps in skill gap methodology.

## Proposed Method
Systematic-narrative hybrid review; PRISMA guidelines; keywords: "skill gap," "skill mismatch," "skill shortage," "identifying or measuring," "Industry 4.0"; 40 articles included; thematic analysis + constant comparisons.

## Key Findings
- **Skill gaps certainly exist** — concerns raised worldwide; growing with digital transformation
- **No consensus definition**: Authors propose: skill gap = A–B = C, where A = skills needed for future tasks, B = skills currently present, C = skills to hire or develop/train
- **Dominant measurement approaches** (in order of frequency in literature): surveys/questionnaires, competency assessments, job posting analysis, education outcome tracking, labour market analytics
- **Survey weaknesses confirmed**: subjectivity, peer positivity bias, self-assessment inaccuracy — workers often don't know their own skill levels
- **Job posting analysis strength**: provides direct employer demand signal; overcomes survey subjectivity; but misses implicit skills and soft skills
- **Multimodal measurement recommended**: no single method is sufficient; combining employer surveys + job posting analysis + competency testing produces most reliable gap estimates
- Industry 4.0 context: technical and digital skills show fastest-growing gaps; social/soft skills are also increasingly valued by employers

## Limitations of This Paper
Industry 4.0 focus — results may not fully generalise to broader HE contexts. English-language records only — non-English literature (including Thai) excluded. Review period ends 2022 — misses recent LLM-era developments. Measurement quality assessment limited by heterogeneity of reported metrics.

## Notes for Iris
**Most useful for framing and limitations discussion.** The multi-method recommendation directly validates our hybrid approach: Iris uses job-posting-derived demand distributions (primary) and optionally employer survey data (future work). The A–B = C formula is the cleanest formal definition of skill gap we've seen in the literature — cite this in our methods section when defining what we measure. The confirmation that surveys have peer positivity bias further justifies why Iris uses job postings (employer-stated requirements) rather than graduate self-assessments as the demand signal.
