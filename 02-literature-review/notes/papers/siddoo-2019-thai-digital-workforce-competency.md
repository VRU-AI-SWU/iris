## Research Question
What are the competencies required by IT-related industries in Thailand for the digital workforce, and how can these competencies be clustered into meaningful categories?

## Limitations of Existing Methods
Existing IT competency models (SFIA, iCD, ETA) are Western-designed frameworks not validated for the Thai labour market context. Prior Thai studies relied on national statistics (headcounts, not skill-level analysis) or broad surveys without validated competency clustering. No Thai-specific empirical competency framework existed at career-path granularity.

## Contribution
First empirically grounded, Thailand-specific digital workforce competency framework. Mixed-method design: expert interviews (Phase 1) → survey (Phase 2) → Exploratory Factor Analysis for clustering. Validated 24 competencies clustered into 3 categories relevant to Thai IT industries.

## Proposed Method
- **Phase 1**: Literature review + 30 semi-structured interviews with IT industry experts → content analysis → 24 competency items identified
- **Phase 2**: 260-questionnaire survey of IT experts (IT industry and IT-in-non-IT industries) → Exploratory Factor Analysis (EFA) with Eigenvalue criterion → 3-factor solution
- Comparison between IT industry vs. IT-in-non-IT industry demand profiles

## Key Findings
- EFA yielded 3 competency categories: **(1) Professional skills & IT knowledge**, **(2) IT technical skills**, **(3) IT management & support**
- Industries most demanded Professional skills & IT knowledge, followed by IT technical, then IT management
- **Top 5 most demanded competencies**: lifelong learning, personal attitude, teamwork, dependability, IT foundations
- IT industry vs. non-IT industry: slight difference in demand profile — non-IT industry emphasises soft/professional skills more
- Confirmed competency mismatch between university graduates and Thai industry expectations — validating the skill gap problem in the Thai context
- National Statistical Office data cited: Thailand produced 130K–149K IT graduates/year (2014–2016) but 13K–18K new IT positions remained unfilled annually

## Limitations of This Paper
Expert-validated competency list (24 items) is broad and not a granular skill taxonomy. EFA-derived clusters do not map to a machine-readable ontology. Sample limited to Chiang Mai / Phuket regions (Chiang Mai University and Prince of Songkla University networks) — not nationally representative. Data from 2017–2018 period.

## Notes for Iris
**High relevance for domain context and validation.** The 3-category competency structure (Professional/Technical/Management) could serve as a coarse validation layer for our segment taxonomy design. The confirmed gap between Thai IT graduates and industry demands is direct motivation for Iris. The 24-competency list provides a human-readable reference point against which our extracted skill clusters can be sanity-checked. Note: this is supply-demand gap evidence from the employer perspective; our approach extracts from job postings rather than surveys, so this paper provides convergent validity rather than direct method input.
